# 24 — The 30-day program

*Fifteen minutes a day, four exit tests, one rule: out loud, every day.*

## The question this chapter answers

Everything before this page is material. Material doesn't interview; reflexes do — and reading, however careful, builds recognition, not recall. This chapter is the conversion schedule: which chapter on which day, which drills, what leaves your mouth, and the four tests that tell you the instinct is actually forming. It assumes nothing on day 1 except [chapter 1](01-the-skill-that-decides-the-round.md) in front of you and a blank calibration log — you can start the program and the book on the same morning.

## The contract

Five terms. They *are* the program; the table below is just their arrangement.

1. **Fifteen minutes a day — twenty on the heavy ones.** Week 3's double walkthroughs and week 4's mocks run twenty; the table's last column is honest about which. Never more: stopping while it's still easy is what makes tomorrow's session happen.
2. **Every day.** The unit of progress is the day, not the hour.
3. **Out loud.** The interview is a spoken format, so the training is too. Whisper if you're in public, but the words leave your mouth.
4. **Answers before peeking.** Attempt every drill cold. The answers are collapsed precisely to make cheating inconvenient.
5. **Log every ratio.** Your answer ÷ the book's, one line per drill, in the calibration log from [chapter 23](23-drills.md). The log is the program's instrument panel; week 4 is steered by it.

Miss a day: do tomorrow what was scheduled today, and shift everything right. Never restart from day 1. Miss two days in a row and the price is roughly a week of consolidation — recalls that were due come back harder, and harder recalls rebuild slower. That's a price list, not a verdict. Pay it and keep walking.

Why daily-small beats weekend-big: estimation is retrieval practice, and retrieval strength grows when a number is pulled from memory just as it begins to fade — spaced, slightly effortful recalls. A three-hour Saturday is one recall wearing the costume of twelve.

The shape is couch-to-5K: nobody runs the distance on day one, and the program's whole trick is that no single day is hard. The difficulty lives in the calendar, not the session.

> ⚠️ **Trap** — The restart loop. Miss day 9, declare a fresh start Monday, meet day 9 again, repeat until the book joins the graveyard of half-finished courses. Restarting feels like rigor; it's the program's only real failure mode. Resume, always.

## The four weeks

Each week has a theme and a binary exit test. Fail a test and you repeat the previous two days, then retake it — the schedule bends, the bar doesn't.

### Week 1 — Anchors & arithmetic (days 1–7)

Chapters [1](01-the-skill-that-decides-the-round.md)–[4](04-the-ladder.md), T1 drills daily, and the [Anki deck](https://github.com/pgcurious/estimation-instinct/blob/main/drills/anki-anchor-numbers.csv) from day 1 at ten new cards a day. The week's only job is to make [the cheat sheet](a-cheat-sheet.md) yours. The sheet itself promises you'll hold it by day 10; the program front-loads that to day 7, because every later week spends these anchors daily.

**You pass week 1 when**, on day 7, you reproduce cheat-sheet §§1–9 on a blank page with at most one slip, and write the eight pocket formulas verbatim. One slip means one: a wrong number, a missing row, a formula you had to grope for. Two or more — repeat days 5–6 and retake.

### Week 2 — The six estimations (days 8–14)

One core chapter a day, [5](05-traffic.md) through [10](10-latency-availability-cost.md), each chased with two T2 drills from that chapter's family. The daily speak-aloud is the chapter's pocket formula plus one worked chain, said the way you'd say it in a room — assumption, arithmetic, so-what.

**You pass week 2 when**, on day 14, you clear six T2 drills — one per estimation, covering all eight pocket formulas between them — every answer within 2× of the book's, none taking longer than 30 seconds. Log all six ratios; week 4 reads them.

### Week 3 — The reps (days 15–21)

Two systems a day, two modes. The first system you **read** cold, then stand up and perform its "Say it in 60 seconds" from memory. The second you **speak** first: open the chapter only to its assumptions table, climb the whole ladder aloud from those numbers, *then* read the chapter to grade yourself. The grading stings exactly where it teaches. These are the program's heaviest days — an honest twenty minutes.

The eleven walkthroughs run in book order, two a day across days 15–19. Day 20 carries one — the [metrics aggregator](21-metrics-aggregator.md), the densest of the eleven — plus a from-memory rerun of whichever system your log says you butchered.

**You pass week 3 when**, on day 21, you generate two random numbers from 11 to 21 and perform both systems' full ladders aloud, five minutes each, book closed, chains unbroken.

### Week 4 — Integration & pressure (days 22–30)

T3 sprints and a T4 Fermi curveball daily, [chapter 22](22-the-interview-script.md) at the top of the week and again the night before the final, and three mock rounds:

- **Mock 1 (day 24)** — a full round, self-recorded. Listen back the same day. Painful; works.
- **Mock 2 (day 26)** — a friend or colleague runs chapter 22's pushback playbook against you. Send the booking message on day 22: the booking, not the mock, is the step people skip.
- **Mock 3 (day 28)** — timed solo, on a T3 system you haven't touched, recorded.

**You pass the program when**, on day 30, you draw three random systems and run all three chains aloud, five minutes each, logged: every chain complete, every number within 3× of defensible, every estimation landing on a so-what.

> 🎯 **In the room** — Day 30's bar is the room's bar. Interviewers don't grade your arithmetic against an answer key; they grade whether each number visibly changed your design. Within 3× with a so-what outscores within 10% with silence — which is exactly why the final is scored this way.

## Habit mechanics

Four mechanics carry the streak; none needs an app.

- **Same time, attached to something that already happens** — with the morning chai, on the commute, right after standup. The program should have a *when*, not a to-do entry.
- **Stand for everything spoken.** It's the posture you'll perform in, and it kills the mumble-read that feels like speaking but isn't.
- **A visible streak mark.** Paper calendar, one X per day. Cheap, slightly silly, effective.
- **The weekly listen-back.** Record one spoken rep a week and play it. You'll hate your pauses, then you'll fix them — the fastest feedback loop in the program.

## After day 30

Two drills a week — one T2, one T4 curveball — keep the edge more or less indefinitely. Anchors that survived thirty days of spaced recalls decay slowly; what rusts first is the *speed* of the spoken chain, and the weekly T2 is what oils it. When an interview lands, run the [reading guide](00-how-to-read-this-book.md)'s 3-day cram path as a booster: blank-page the cheat sheet twice, perform three walkthroughs aloud, [chapter 22](22-the-interview-script.md) the night before.

> **The night before any interview**
>
> - Reproduce [the cheat sheet](a-cheat-sheet.md) from a blank page — once is enough.
> - Perform one walkthrough's 60-second close aloud, standing. Pick the system nearest the company's domain.
> - Reread [chapter 22](22-the-interview-script.md)'s pushback playbook.
> - Sleep. An extra hour of sleep beats an extra hour of drills.

## Numbers to keep

- Fifteen minutes × 30 days; the table marks the twenty-minute days
- Miss one day: shift right and resume. Miss two: ~a week of consolidation. Never restart.
- Day 7: cheat-sheet §§1–9 from a blank page, ≤1 slip; 8 formulas verbatim
- Day 14: six T2 — each within 2×, under 30 s
- Day 21: two random systems, full ladders aloud, 5 min each, book closed
- Day 30: three random systems — chains complete, within 3×, every number lands a so-what
- After: 2 drills/week, plus the 3-day booster before any interview

## Day 31

The instinct is now maintenance-cheap — two drills a week keep what took thirty days to build. The one thing this book can't simulate is a stranger watching, and you've already rehearsed that three times this week. Book the real mock you've been avoiding. Today.
