# How to read this book

*This is a training manual, not a textbook. The difference is in what you do with your mouth.*

## The speak-aloud rule

Estimation in an interview is a **verbal, real-time performance**. You will not be writing in silence; you'll be talking while multiplying, with someone watching. So train the way you'll perform:

- Every worked example in this book: cover the math, **say your own version out loud**, then compare.
- Every drill: answer aloud *before* opening the collapsed answer. No exceptions — the collapse exists to make cheating inconvenient.
- Every walkthrough in Part III ends with **"Say it in 60 seconds"**. Perform it. Standing up helps, oddly.

Reading silently builds recognition — "yes, that looks right." Speaking builds recall — producing numbers from nothing, under mild stress. Interviews test recall. The single biggest reason engineers freeze on estimation despite "knowing the material" is that they trained recognition and got tested on recall.

## Three paths through the book

**The 3-day path (interview imminent).**
Read chapters [2](part-1-foundations/02-the-arithmetic-of-scale.md), [3](part-1-foundations/03-the-numbers-that-matter.md), [4](part-1-foundations/04-the-ladder.md). Memorize [the cheat sheet](appendices/a-cheat-sheet.md) — actually memorize it; blank-page-reproduce it twice. Do walkthroughs [11](part-3-walkthroughs/11-url-shortener.md), [12](part-3-walkthroughs/12-news-feed.md), and [14](part-3-walkthroughs/14-video-platform.md) aloud. Read [the interview script](part-4-instinct/22-the-interview-script.md) the night before. This gets you to *competent* — visibly trained, occasionally slow.

**The 30-day path (the one that builds instinct).**
Follow [the 30-day program](part-4-instinct/24-the-30-day-program.md): fifteen minutes a day, chapters and drills interleaved, every walkthrough performed twice. Instinct is a spaced-repetition outcome — there is no 8-hour-Saturday substitute, the same way there's no 8-hour-Saturday route to a 5K time.

**The reference path (already comfortable, want sharpening).**
Cheat sheet to recalibrate anchors, Part III for unfamiliar system shapes, chapter [23](part-4-instinct/23-drills.md) for the Fermi curveballs, chapter [22](part-4-instinct/22-the-interview-script.md) for the room-craft you can't get from arithmetic.

## What this book is not

This is not a full system design book. It will not teach you consistent hashing, consensus, or how to draw the boxes — it teaches the **numbers that decide which boxes you need**. Pair it with any system design course you like; this book is the missing organ, not a competing body.

It is also not a mathematics book. If you can multiply single digits and add exponents, you have all the math. What feels like math anxiety in estimation is almost always *missing anchors* plus *no procedure* — both fixable, neither talent.

## The deal

The book keeps its end: every number traces to [one canonical page](appendices/a-cheat-sheet.md), every formula is derived before it's used, every chapter follows the same skeleton so the moves become muscle memory.

Your end: out loud, every time, answers before peeking. Thirty days from now, someone asks "roughly how much storage?" and a number leaves your mouth before you've decided to speak. That's the instinct. It's just reps.
